MyKlemis App

The MyKlemis App is a working application built for Georgia Tech's on campus food pantry, Klemis Kitchen, as part of the Junior Design class sequence. Klemis Kitchen aims to serve as an alternate source of meals for students who have special dietary needs or are experiencing financial concerns that cause them to be food insecure.

As of now, Klemis Kitchen currently does not have an application or an inventory management system that both students and admins can use to keep track of what items are available. Furthermore, Klemis Kitchen plans to open new outposts locations across campus and this can be difficult without some sort of app or way to keep organized about the inventory across the locations.

The MyKlemis App helps fill this gap by allowing admins and students to be able to easily view Klemis Kitchen’s entire inventory and also allows admins an easier way to help keep track and make changes to the inventory.

Brainstorming & Research

We started off with brainstorming our possible users for the application and grouping them into general groups. The team came up with five possible users:

  • Procurement: users who help to gather or donate items for Klemis Kitchen
  • Consumers: users, such as students, who use Klemis Kitchen to obtain items
  • Management: users such as admins and faculty who manage Klemis Kitchen
  • Giving Back: users such as volunteers who help Klemis Kitchen, but aren’t admins
  • Unintended Usage: free riders/ people who don’t need use Klemis Kitchen

The last user group, Unintended Usage was scrapped as the team felt that it didn’t make much sense. Our team then went and conducted literature reviews for the four possible user groups we had.



Prototyping & Design

When we first began sketching wireframes and possible screens, we mistakenly believed that the application would have a login for both administrators and students, and that students could add food items to a cart and “check” them out using the app. We also planned for a chat feature that would allow students to discuss problems and food items with administrators, as well as a home landing page that would serve as a notice board holding announcements on upcoming deliveries as well as low food amounts.



Some initial designs we had before the team realized changes needed to be made. You can see how students were also originally meant to log in and be able to check out food items, but this feature was scrapped for privacy concerns.


It wasn’t until the team got to physically tour Klemis Kitchen’s facilities that we realized our current design didn’t fit the client’s actual needs and that it needed to go through some changes. The team’s understanding of how Klemis Kitchen operated was much different from how it actually operated, and we realized that we were focusing on the wrong things for the app.

Our literature review also revealed that according to a small survey conducted at UNT, the majority of the food insecure participants stated they would use a campus food pantry, but also expressed fear about being seen by other fellow students and the need for privacy.

To ensure privacy, we removed the student login, and allowed anyone to view Klemis Kitchen’s inventory. A separate login was made for admins to be able to make edits and announcements. The chat idea was scrapped in favor of an email form that users could use to get in touch with Klemis Kitchen admins.


When a user opens up the app, they will be greeted by the Home page which will display announcements made by the Klemis Kitchen admins. The left image shows the admin's view, and the right image shows the student view.



Users can view a list of the locations Klemis Kitchen has by clicking on the "Locations" option in the hamburger menu. Admin users (left two images) have the option to edit the list and make any necessary changes. Clicking on a location brings up more information about the location.



Users can view a list of the items Klemis Kitchen has. Admins (left) have the added option to change quantities of items through the plus and minus buttons or through manually typing in a number into the text box.



When a student user navigates to the “Help” page, they will be first greeted with the screen shown above (first screen for Android; third screen for IOS). If a student would like to send and email, they can click on the email icon, which will open the default email application on the student’s phone, with the Klemis Kitchen contact email filled out in the recipient textfield (middle screen). From here, the user can fill out the subject and body of their email and send it to Klemis Kitchen as they normally would with the email application.




Programming

Programming was done in five three-week sprints with team members assigned to work on specific tasks during each sprint. The final app was programmed using Xamarin with an Amazon Web Services (AWS) backend in addition to Klemis Kitchen's own backend. I helped work on the front end of the app, and helped to program several of the app screens.
